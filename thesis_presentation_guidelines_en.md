# Thesis Presentation Slides — Instructions & Guidelines

Based on the Greek lecture PDF **“Ανάπτυξη Διπλωματικής Εργασίας — Διάλεξη 03”**.

## 1. Purpose of the Thesis Presentation

The thesis presentation is an oral overview of your thesis research. It usually lasts around **15 minutes or more** and focuses on the study, methodology, findings, and conclusions presented in the corresponding thesis chapters.

The main goals are to:

- Present and highlight the work you completed.
- Demonstrate your oral presentation skills.
- Show that you understand your research topic deeply.
- Receive additional feedback from the thesis committee.
- Defend and explain your research choices, findings, and conclusions.

A typical thesis presentation should contain approximately **10–20 slides**, prepared using presentation software such as **PowerPoint** or **LaTeX**.

## 2. General Presentation Principles

### Think about the audience

The presentation should be designed around what the audience needs in order to understand your work. Do not simply copy text from the thesis. Select, simplify, and organize the information so that it can be understood quickly during an oral presentation.

### Use storytelling

Treat the presentation as a clear story of your research journey:

1. What problem did you investigate?
2. Why is it important?
3. What does the literature say?
4. What gap did you identify?
5. What research questions did you ask?
6. What methodology did you use?
7. What did you find?
8. What do the findings mean?
9. What are the implications and limitations?

### Keep slides short and focused

Slide space is limited. Each slide should be simple, clear, and purposeful. Avoid overcrowding slides with too much text or too many details.

A good slide should usually contain:

- One main idea.
- Short bullet points.
- Visual support where useful.
- Clear connection to the overall argument of the thesis.

## 3. Preparation Steps

## Step 1 — Collect the material

Before creating the slides, make sure you clearly understand your thesis and collect all material that may be useful, such as:

- Key points from each chapter.
- Important definitions.
- Research questions.
- Methodology details.
- Tables, charts, diagrams, and figures.
- Important results.
- Key references.
- Conclusions and recommendations.

## Step 2 — Plan the presentation

Create a structure before opening PowerPoint or LaTeX. Decide:

- How many slides you will use.
- What each slide will contain.
- In what order the information will be presented.
- Which parts need visuals, tables, or diagrams.
- Which details should be spoken orally instead of written on the slide.

## Step 3 — Choose the presentation tool

You may use:

- **Microsoft PowerPoint**, using either a template or a blank presentation.
- **LaTeX/Beamer**, especially for more academic or technical presentations.

Choose the tool that allows you to present your work clearly and professionally.

## Step 4 — Create the slides

Build the slides according to your planned structure. Keep each slide simple and understandable. Avoid filling the slide with long paragraphs.

## Step 5 — Format the presentation

Use formatting to make the presentation attractive and readable, but avoid unnecessary decoration.

You may use:

- Consistent fonts.
- Clear headings.
- Balanced spacing.
- Tables and charts.
- Simple backgrounds.
- Limited animations or transitions.

Transitions and animations should support the presentation, not distract from it.

## 4. Recommended Slide Structure

The structure of the presentation should roughly follow the table of contents of the thesis, but it should not be a full chapter-by-chapter copy. It should give a concise and linear overview of the research.

## Slide 1 — Title Slide

Include:

- Thesis title.
- Student name.
- Supervisor name.
- Program/department/university.
- Date of presentation.

The title slide should clearly introduce the topic and create a professional first impression.

## Slide 2 — Topic Background

Explain the general background of the topic.

Answer questions such as:

- What is the broader field of the thesis?
- Why is this topic important?
- What motivated this research?
- What practical or academic problem does it relate to?

More than one slide may be used if the topic background is complex.

## Slide 3 — Research Gap / Motivation

Explain why this research was needed.

Include:

- The gap in existing research.
- The missing or insufficiently explored issue.
- Why your thesis contributes something useful.

This slide should help the audience understand why your thesis matters.

## Slide 4 — Problem Statement

Clearly state the problem your thesis addresses.

The problem statement should explain the reason for the study in one or two clear sentences.

A useful structure is:

> The purpose of this study is to...

This statement should reflect the purpose of your thesis.

## Slide 5 — Significance of the Research

Explain the importance of your work.

Answer:

- How can your research affect the field of study?
- Why are the findings important?
- Who may benefit from this research?
- What is the academic or practical value of the thesis?

## Slide 6 — Theoretical Background

Include a brief slide on the theoretical orientation of the thesis.

This may include:

- Main theories used.
- Key concepts.
- Relevant models or frameworks.
- Academic foundations of the study.

Keep this section concise. The goal is to show understanding, not to repeat the full literature review.

## Slide 7 — Conceptual Framework / Literature Overview

Present the main categories of literature used in the thesis.

Include:

- Main research areas connected to the topic.
- What previous studies have shown.
- Important observations from the literature.
- Gaps that your study attempts to address.

Mention authors and years correctly when referring to literature.

## Slide 8 — Research Questions

Present the main research question and any secondary questions.

Guidelines:

- Number the questions for easy reference.
- Keep them clear and concise.
- Make sure they are connected to the literature gap.
- Use the same research questions that guide your methodology and findings.

Example format:

- RQ1: ...
- RQ2: ...
- RQ3: ...

## Slide 9 — Methodology Overview

Define the main research methodology used in the thesis.

Explain:

- What type of research you conducted.
- Why this methodology was appropriate.
- How it helped answer the research questions.
- Which methodological literature supports your choice.

## Slide 10+ — Methodology Details

Use several slides if needed for methodology details.

Possible methodology slides:

### Participants / Sample

Include:

- Number of participants or observations.
- Selection process.
- Relevant demographic or dataset characteristics.

### Data Collection

Explain:

- How data was collected.
- What tools, sources, or instruments were used.
- Why these sources were suitable.

### Data Analysis

Explain:

- How the data was analyzed.
- What models, techniques, or procedures were used.
- How the analysis connects to the research questions.

### Reliability and Validity

Mention how you ensured that the research was credible, reliable, and valid.

### Limitations

Briefly state the main limitations of the methodology or available data.

## Results / Findings Slides

This section usually requires multiple slides.

Guidelines:

- Use the same organizational structure as the results chapter of the thesis.
- Present enough detail to be convincing.
- Do not present so much detail that the audience gets lost.
- Use tables, charts, and diagrams from the thesis where useful.
- Highlight the most important findings.
- Explain how each finding relates to the research questions.

A table may be the best way to present some results, especially when comparing multiple findings, models, groups, or metrics.

## Conclusions Slide

Summarize what you learned from the study.

Answer:

- What are the main conclusions?
- How do the findings relate to the conceptual framework or literature?
- What do the results mean?
- What are the strengths of the work?
- What are the weaknesses or limitations?

## Implications Slide

Explain the implications of your research.

Include:

- Recommendations for future research.
- Two or three new research questions that emerged during the study.
- Practical or theoretical implications.
- Possible policy or practice recommendations, if relevant.

## Recommendations Slide

Depending on the thesis topic, recommendations may refer to:

- Changes in theoretical frameworks.
- Public policy.
- Business or organizational practices.
- Future technical or methodological improvements.

## References Slide

Include only the references that are actually mentioned in the presentation.

Guidelines:

- Use full bibliographic details.
- One slide is usually enough.
- Do not include the entire thesis bibliography unless all sources are used in the presentation.

## Final Slide — Thank You / Questions

End with a slide thanking:

- The thesis committee.
- The supervisor.
- The audience.

Use the final slide to invite questions from the committee.

Example:

> Thank you for your attention.  
> Questions?

## 5. Suggested Slide Order

A practical slide order for a 10–20 slide thesis presentation is:

1. Title slide
2. Topic background
3. Research motivation / gap
4. Problem statement
5. Significance of the research
6. Theoretical background
7. Literature overview / conceptual framework
8. Research questions
9. Methodology overview
10. Data / sample / participants
11. Data collection
12. Data analysis method
13. Results — key finding 1
14. Results — key finding 2
15. Results — key finding 3
16. Discussion of findings
17. Conclusions
18. Implications / recommendations
19. References
20. Thank you / questions

For a shorter presentation, combine related sections. For example, background and motivation can be one slide, or implications and recommendations can be one slide.

## 6. Practical Design Guidelines

### Do

- Use clear and readable fonts.
- Keep each slide focused on one purpose.
- Use charts, tables, and diagrams where they make the message clearer.
- Highlight the most important findings.
- Keep text short and speak the explanation orally.
- Maintain a consistent visual style.
- Number research questions for easy discussion.
- Cite authors and years correctly when referring to literature.
- Connect results back to the research questions.
- End with a strong final impression.

### Avoid

- Long paragraphs copied from the thesis.
- Too many bullet points on one slide.
- Overly detailed tables that cannot be read quickly.
- Excessive animations or transitions.
- Unclear connection between research questions, methodology, and findings.
- Including references that were not mentioned in the presentation.
- Presenting every thesis detail instead of the most important points.

## 7. Key Reminder

The presentation is your final opportunity to make a strong impression on the thesis committee. It should be clear, concise, visually organized, and focused on showing that you understand your topic, your methodology, and the value of your findings.
